The core point

Rents were kept low for years. That was a choice — and it has a cost.

Keeping rents under market kept good long-term tenants in place. It also capped the building's income, and a building's value follows its income. The numbers below show the gap, and the plan to close it on the renovated units while honoring existing tenants.

Current vs. market

The gap, by the rent roll.

$20,911

$38,035

monthly gap — roughly $205,000 a year

Part of that gap is vacancy (8 units, closed by lease-up). Part is in-place rents that sit under market on long-term leases. As an illustration: at typical Seattle small-building cap rates, every $1,000 a month of added rent supports on the order of $200,000 of building value. So the building is worth less because rents are low?

Yes. Value follows income. Below-market rents were a deliberate trade-off the family made, and reversing it on turnover (not on existing tenants' backs) is the plan on this page.

The premise

Spend where renters actually look. Skip the rest.

Eight units to lease means marketing is a near-term spend with a measurable return: cost per lease. Here's where we recommend putting money, what each channel should return, and one channel we recommend skipping.

Recommended channels

Three to fund, one to skip.

Recommended

Zillow package

Where the largest share of Seattle renters search first. Paid placement keeps our 8 availabilities at the top of the feed. ROI framing: cost ÷ expected leases = cost per lease, against one month's rent.

Recommended

Google drip / digital campaign

Search and display ads targeting "apartments Northgate / Pinehurst" intent, pointed at the building's own website. Builds a lead pipeline we own rather than rent.

Recommended

EDDM / neighborhood mailer

Every-door direct mail in surrounding routes. Cheap per household, and renters often move within the neighborhood they already live in.

Not recommended

Apartments.com

Its audience and pricing skew toward large professionally-amenitized complexes; for a 21-unit building the cost per qualified lead doesn't pencil. State the specific numbers here.
